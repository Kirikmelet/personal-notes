# Personal Statement

I was born to two undocumented parents, and later I was joined by my sister.
My parents were Indonesian, both college-educated, yet finding a job for them
was somewhat a challenge. Thankfully my father was able to secure semi-regular
freelancing projects that paid really well, and my mother worked 10-hour-shifts, under-the-table
jobs almost every day; the income made by my parents admittedly allowed us to live
a decent middle-class lifestyle. But in mid-2015, our grandmothers were starting to
show age, and illness. Our parents made the though choice to move to Indonesia.

Living in Indonesia was an eye-opening experience for me. There, I encountered
true poverty for the first time. In school, I was mocked for being a "fake American",
because I had the same ethnicity as my peers. Academic life in Asia was a completely
different experience than in the US, and my grades started to deteriorate slightly as
a result. I lived in Indonesia for five years, but towards the end, my parents pulled me
out of school because my father's business wasn't doing so well at the time.
However, my parents wanted me to continue my education, and after finishing a work contract,
my parents sent me back to the USA to continue my education.

I lived with my mother's former boss in Arlington Heights, and I re-entered high school a year and half behind than
originally planned. At first it was a little weird, but I managed to do well in class.
But after that semester, my guardian was too stressed and became unable to care for me,
so I moved in with my godmother's sibling. I had to move to Altanta, Georgia.
It was the first time I lived in the US outside of the midwest, and I had to adjust
to the different demographic and climate. But my father passed away suddenly to a
heart attack. And I had to find a job to make allowance. Working a job and being in
high school taught me how to balance my time and tasks, but it was a difficult
challenge for me. I found it hard to complete my homework and projects after/before
work, coupled also that I joined my school's NJROTC unit. NJROTC was a great
experience for me, the program was also the main activity I was looking
forward to doing in school. I was a member for our unit's drill team, I was a class
leader, and I actively participated in unit inspections and tournaments;
I was even supposed to be one of our drill team's 2ic. On the other hand, I was
continuously fatigued, and I often overslept or fell asleep in class.
School went fine however, but life had other plans.

My guardian at the time didn't really like me - particularly after I was too loud at
night playing video games and after I lost my key - and kicked me out. I had to
return to Indonesia, after using up my entire savings. I resided with my mother, sister,
and family dog.  
A family friend offered to take me in, and I moved in with them. They lived in Ames, Iowa.
In Ames, I had to find a job (again), and I had to go to school. School was something I was excited about,
work was not so much. I balanced my responsibilities again between work and school. But the stress
from balancing it was difficult, and I was still often fatigued asleep. I made friends too!
Two of them were exchange students; one from Poland, one from Germany. Their different backgrounds
were certainly interesting to learn about. Additionally, I was the co-host for my German friend.
And although I might say I learnt German from them, they spoke primarily in English.
Nonetheless, my guardian  was going through a divorce and withal made me return to Indonesia again;
yet again I had to use my entire savings.

In Indonesia I tried to self-learn math and Japanese, albeit to various results.
And eight months later, and after over-staying my visa.  My visa overstay
was a bit of an issue; legally I had to be detained for thirty or more days before deportation.
I turned myself in to the immigration authorities, fully prepared to face the full
extant of the law. The immigration officers however, were corrupt, and my grandmother
bribed the officers to skip the detention period. It was interesting witnessing first-hand corruption.

I returned to Chicago in time before my eighteenth birthday, I stayed with another family friend.
But I knew I had to quickly find my own place to stay, find a job to make income to sustain myself,
and also to complete my secondary education. I thought about high school, but because of time obligations,
I opted instead to take a GED. I was able to find a room to rent, and a job at Jewel-Osco. And in
the down-time in-between my work shifts, I studyed for the GED. Another challenge at the time was
that my mother was running out of savings to fund my sister's education, so I had to portion some of my savings
so my sister could continue her education. And despite my challenges and obligations,
on the day I took my GED, I passed successfully with flying colors! I was proud of it.

I applied to Harper College starting the summer of 2024, and I took advantage of the fact that
my income was below the SAI threshold. This gave me full PELL grants to start my post-secondary education.
It was a bit of a challenge for me to attend college, because I didn't have a driver's license yet,
and I only had a bike. Furthermore, I had then recently moved to Schaumburg, and I had to commute
one hour each way to work, and thirty minutes to college. And so I had to carefully plan my classes
and work hours in order for me to appease all my responsibilities. My early classes weren't too hard,
but I faced my first obstacle in Chemistry, I underestimated my work and I had other goings in life.
I started to learn to drive. It didn't take me too long to learn to drive and get my license;
afterwards I used some of my FAFSA refund to buy my own car. Not even a week later, I got into my first accident,
I was fortunate the victims did not care enough to file insurance, but I was taught a very important lesson in
driving carefully. I caused the accident while I was Doordashing, because I was living paycheck-to-paycheck
 by despite my full-time job. However, I had to wisen up, and focus on my classes. I was too late, I finished the semester
with my very first C. I had a C in Chemistry class. Looking back, I acknowledged that I studied too little,
and I even though I had my things going on a the time, it wasn't an excuse to slack on my studies. On the other hand
the stress of buying a car before it got cold and financial stress of affording a vacation back to Indonesia for family reasons
did take a toll on my mental health. I carried on, and I recovered my grades the next semester.

Following that semester, I returned to Indonesia for a brief time and to bring my sister to live with me in Chicago so
she can continue her education here. I failed to notice though, that my summer class in Calculus II started while I was
in still in Indonesia, and the very first day I was back in Chicago I had to take a test I was woefully ill-prepared for.
The added stressor of learning to be a guardian and handling paperwork while also at the same time working a full-time job
made it difficult for me to study the accelerated summer course. I finished with an F. I was incredibly distraught at this,
but I was fortunate enough to have my friends and mother (via WhatsApp) to confide in and fall back to.
Despite that, I pushed to do better next time. I finished my sister's paperwork to allow her to start high school and medical work.
In September of that year - 2025 - I had to bring her to the ER because of her mental trauma. She got diagnosed with a major depressive
disorder and ADHD; at the same time I had a separate consultation and got diagnosed with ADHD. My diagnosis brought a strange calm for
me, because it help explain why I found it difficult to study and concentrate at times, and my social activity  as noted by my peers.

That same semester started be active in clubs, I was (and am) the vice-president of Harper College's Computer Science Club,
an active member of the college's chapter of the Young Democratic Socialists of America - where I helped with promoting social justice.
That same semester I again made the same mistake of undestimating my phyiscs class, and I ended with a D.
After that same semester I did not let that hamper my dream of finished college, and steeled my resolve. 

Continuing to now, I am retaking my calculus II class, and I am focusing hard to study as much as possible.
I am enjoying learning German, because my dream after graduating UIC is to study abroad in Germany for graduate school.
I have been taking care of my sister, taking full financial and material obligations too; and I still continue to assist my mother financially.

Despite my setbacks in grades, I did not, and I will not let that impact my willingness to retake and finished the class with a grade
I am satisfied with. In applying to UIC, I hope to be able to finish my degree with my head held high - fully knowing
that despite working a full-time job, full-time college, full-time guardian, I have managed to persevere.
